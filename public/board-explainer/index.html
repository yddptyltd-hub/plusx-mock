Q3 Plan: 12 Weeks to Launch OwnX

The 3 systems, the Friday meeting, the Sunday rhythm.

Why this meeting exists

Section 1 — Why this meeting exists

Artem is calling this meeting because we have a problem. We keep saying we’ll do things and then not doing them. Not because anyone is lazy — because we don’t have systems. Everything depends on whichever cofounder is in the room that day. He wants to fix that by introducing structured meetings, templates everyone fills in the same way, and a visible scorecard so the gap between what we said and what we did stops hiding.

The big picture: we have three months. End of summer, OwnX needs to launch. Right now it can’t, because LPX needs founders on every call. So those three months are about building the LPX systems that let LPX run without us, freeing us to build OwnX.

Goal 1

Section 2 — Goal 1: Manager onboarding and training

Goal 1 is a system that turns a stranger into a working LPX manager without us in the room. The number of managers is the score; the system is the work.

Four questions you answer to design it:

One — what does a manager actually do day to day? Explain NTZ width to a depositor, walk a customer through the dashboard, read TVL changes and react, decide when to widen or narrow a pool, handle support questions. Write that list first. Until it’s written, you can’t teach anyone.

Two — for each thing on that list, what’s the cheapest way to put it in someone’s head? Some of it is reading. Some is watching a short video of you doing a real call. Some is doing under supervision — the new manager runs a call, you watch silently. Pick the cheapest method per skill.

Three — how do you know they’re ready? Some kind of gate. They shadow three calls, run three with you on mute, then pass. Or a quiz on the methodology numbers. Or a real funded pool they manage solo for a week and the TVL holds. Pick something measurable.

Four — what’s the maintenance loop? Weekly call with all managers, someone owns it, shared doc where they ask questions, you update the playbook when a new edge case shows up.

Goal 2

Section 3 — Goal 2: Weekly A/B TVL experiments

Goal 2 is about ending guesswork. Right now TVL grows or shrinks and we don’t really know why. We try a tactic, something happens, we don’t know if the tactic caused it or if it was random.

The fix is a discipline: every week, exactly one experiment. Hypothesis on paper. Two versions — A and B. One number we’re trying to move. One success rule written before we start, not invented after. One owner. Start and end date.

Four design questions:

One — what counts as TVL? Sounds obvious but it’s not. Is it what’s inside our contracts, or what’s swappable through us? Without a written definition, every weekly experiment is fighting over what the score means.

Two — what’s the experiment template? One page. Hypothesis, A vs B, segment, primary metric, success rule, owner, dates. Same template every week, no variation.

Three — what’s the decision rule? When do we call a winner. How long do we wait. What happens to the loser — kill it, or keep it as a fallback.

Four — how do we attribute? When TVL moves up two percent next month, we need to point at a specific experiment and say this caused it. The weekly TVL report has to say Experiment X contributed N dollars.

Goal 3

Section 4 — Goal 3: X (Twitter) messaging twelve-week plan

Goal 3 is about the public narrative. Right now X posts are high-effort and low-system. Whoever has time posts something. No plan, no themes, no cadence, no measurement.

Artem wants a playbook. Three or four core narratives we hammer on — like LPX equals volatility yield, LPX to uDEX to OwnX is the connection, RWA and asset protection, proofs of real customers not screenshots of dashboards. Each week one main theme plus one supporting proof piece — a real chart, a real number, a real testimonial.

A weekly minimum: a number of posts and a number of replies per cofounder. Standard CTA at the end of every post — learn more, see methodology, however we want to drive people.

Plus a legal piece — what we cannot say. No investment advice, no specific return promises, no fake testimonials.

And the connection: each week’s X content references that week’s TVL experiment. So the public narrative and the internal experiment are the same story.

How every goal ships

Section 5 — How every goal ships: designed, tested, implemented

All three goals move through the same three stages over twelve weeks.

Designed — the playbook or template exists on paper. By the end of week four for each goal.

Tested — we ran it for real. For manager onboarding, that’s at least two new managers through the playbook. For TVL experiments, four consecutive weeks of one A/B per week with written post-mortems. For X messaging, four weeks of posting on plan and each post linked to a TVL experiment. By the end of week eight.

Implemented — the system runs without us. New managers can onboard the next new manager from the doc. Two TVL experiments have graduated into standard play. Any cofounder opens the X playbook on Sunday and plans their week in fifteen minutes. By the end of week twelve.

The Friday meeting

Section 6 — The Friday meeting

Two hours. Six steps, repeated for each goal.

Step one — Artem opens for five minutes. Says where we are, where we want to be, presents the goal.

Step two — each cofounder takes ten minutes to propose their way to hit it. Presented like you’re being graded. Concrete. Measurable. Outcome-focused.

Step three — ten to fifteen minutes of disagreeing productively. One question only: which path is most likely to produce the most output toward the goal? No ego. No theory. Just impact and effort.

Step four — ten minutes to vote and commit. From the winning path we write must-happens — non-negotiable — and nice-to-haves — if we have capacity.

Step five — five minutes to assign one owner for the goal and owners for each must-happen task. The owner reports progress; they don’t do everything alone.

Step six — close. Confirm the plan, knowing it can change, with one agreed way, must-happens, and owners.

By Thursday night you bring a one-pager. Five sections, one page, plain text, no slides.

One — your lane. The area you own.

Two — your twelve-week target. One measurable sentence. By week twelve we have five active LPX managers each running at least one funded pool is Artem’s example.

Three — three concrete outcomes by week twelve. All measurable. Manager playbook published. Onboarding tested with three new managers. Weekly manager call with seventy percent attendance.

Four — three to five weekly actions you own every week to hit those outcomes. Outreach. Calls. Content. UI changes.

Five — risks and asks. What could block you, what you’ll do about it, what you need from other people.

There’s no right plan. Plans change. The point is showing up with thinking on paper so Friday is about choosing and sequencing, not starting from zero.

Every Sunday going forward, you fill in one short block tied to the twelve-week plan.

Top three actions this week in your lane. Ship X, run Y calls, record Z video.

That’s it. Five minutes. Sunday. Every week. Over twelve weeks this becomes a habit, not a chore. Consistent first, exceptional second.

Accountability framework + long-term standard

Section 9 — The accountability framework + the long-term standard

Cofounders are self-managing adults. Accountability means you make a clear written commitment, we measure if it happened, we talk about the gap without drama. No one wants to police anyone — the system polices itself because everyone uses the same template and the scorecard is visible to all. We become consistent first, then exceptional. We have to make these systems work on us before they can work on LPX managers, and eventually on OwnX managers.

The long-term standard, the stretch we’re building toward:

One — treat every meeting like a board meeting. Pre-reads. Tight agenda. Prepared remarks. Clear decisions. Clear actions.

Two — meetings without an agenda are disrespectful, including one-on-ones. No agenda means you didn’t think the other person’s time was worth preparing for.

Three — if you have a slot on the agenda, prepare to deliver it like you’re being graded. Clear framing. Tight talking points. A doc or slide ready to share.

Four — you know your presentation missed when people start asking basic questions or questions come from every direction with no through-line. Stop, say let me rewrite this and we’ll discuss tomorrow, do not let it drag on.

Five — pulling up a document during a meeting that no one has seen before is bad form. If you could have sent it beforehand, you should have.

This is not where we are now. But when we have dozens of OwnX managers, we’ll be grateful we built it.
